# Thumbnail Composition Templates

These are **structural layouts** — the skeleton of the image. The Species brand styling (dark background, red accent, CRT texture) gets applied on top automatically by the prompt system.

Each template is a distinct compositional structure that works for any topic.

---

## SPLIT / COMPARISON FAMILY

### 1. Split Screen (2 panels)
Two things side by side, divided down the middle. Could be contrasting, complementary, or competing. The dividing line itself can be clean, jagged, or energetic.
**When to use:** Any "this vs that" topic. Two competing technologies, two approaches, two outcomes.
**Example:** Left panel = human brain, Right panel = AI chip

### 2. Before / After
Left side is the old state, right side is the new state. Time flows left to right. The difference between the two sides tells the story.
**When to use:** Any transformation, evolution, or change over time. "How X changed everything."
**Example:** Left = office full of workers, Right = empty office with one robot

### 3. Triptych (3 panels)
Three panels side by side showing a progression, a choice, or three aspects of one topic. The middle panel often gets emphasis (slightly larger or brighter).
**When to use:** Three options, three stages, "the good, the bad, and the ugly" format.
**Example:** Three stages of AI: helpful assistant → autonomous agent → superintelligence

### 4. Grid (4+ panels)
Four or more panels in a grid showing variety, range, or collection. Works as 2x2, 2x3, or irregular grid. Each panel is a different example or variation.
**When to use:** "The top 4 tools," roundups, comparisons across many options, showcasing variety.
**Example:** 2x2 grid of four different AI-generated images showing quality differences

### 5. Spectrum / Gradient
Not a hard split but a smooth transition from one state to another across the frame. Left edge is one extreme, right edge is the other, with a continuous gradient between them.
**When to use:** Nuance topics, "it's not black and white," scales, ranges.
**Example:** Left = fully human art, gradually morphing into fully AI art on the right

## SEQUENCE / PROGRESSION FAMILY

### 6. Evolution Line
3-5 objects arranged left to right, each one a step forward from the last. Clear visual escalation — each step is bigger, more complex, or more intense than the previous.
**When to use:** Evolution, progression, "levels," rankings from low to high.
**Example:** GPT-1 (tiny) → GPT-2 → GPT-3 → GPT-4 → GPT-5 (massive, partially hidden)

### 7. Domino / Chain Reaction
A cascade in progress — each element triggering the next, with each step getting bigger. Reads left to right (or top to bottom). The final domino is either massive or cut off by the frame.
**When to use:** Cascading consequences, butterfly effect, "one thing leads to another."
**Example:** Small code change → server crash → company failure → industry collapse

### 8. Timeline / Path
A visual path, road, or timeline receding into the distance. Markers or milestones along the path. The endpoint is either visible (dramatic destination) or disappears into uncertainty.
**When to use:** Roadmaps, history, predictions, journey narratives.
**Example:** A path with year markers (2024, 2025, 2026...) leading into a glowing red horizon

### 9. Funnel / Narrowing
Many things at the top funneling down to one thing at the bottom (or many on the left narrowing to one on the right). The visual compression implies filtering, competition, or convergence.
**When to use:** "Which one wins," elimination, narrowing down options, distillation.
**Example:** 20 AI companies at top → funnel → 1 winner at bottom

### 10. Stacking / Layers
Things stacked vertically — each layer adding to the previous one. Can be literal (blocks, platforms) or conceptual (building up complexity). The top is the newest/most advanced.
**When to use:** Building complexity, foundations, "built on top of," tech stack explanations.
**Example:** Hardware layer → OS layer → AI layer → Application layer → User layer

## SCALE / RELATIONSHIP FAMILY

### 11. Giant vs Tiny
One massive thing and one tiny thing in the same frame. The scale difference IS the message. Not a sequence — just two things, radically different sizes.
**When to use:** Power imbalance, David vs Goliath, being dwarfed by something bigger.
**Example:** Tiny human standing before a massive AI entity filling the sky

### 12. Collision / Impact
Two things about to crash into each other, or at the moment of impact. Motion lines, energy at the contact point, debris. The force of the collision is the message.
**When to use:** Conflict, disruption, "when X meets Y," clashing forces.
**Example:** A wrecking ball labeled "AI" about to hit a building labeled with the old way

### 13. Surrounded / Encircled
One thing in the center, surrounded by many things closing in from all sides. The center element is isolated, possibly threatened, possibly the source.
**When to use:** Being overwhelmed, under attack, surrounded by options, one vs many.
**Example:** A human figure in the center, AI agents closing in from every direction

### 14. Dwarfed by Environment
A small figure in a vast, overwhelming space. Not facing a specific object (that's Giant vs Tiny) — lost in an entire environment. The emptiness/vastness is the point.
**When to use:** Feeling small, being overwhelmed by scale, exploring the unknown, loneliness.
**Example:** Tiny person in an infinite server room stretching in all directions

## REVEAL / MYSTERY FAMILY

### 15. Iceberg / Hidden Depths
Something visible at the surface, with much more hidden below. The revealed structure below the surface is larger and more complex than what's showing. Classic "you only see the tip" format.
**When to use:** Hidden complexity, "what you don't know," deeper than it looks, iceberg explainers.
**Example:** ChatGPT interface visible above waterline, massive infrastructure below

### 16. Cross-Section / Cutaway
Something sliced open to show its internal structure. The exterior is simple; the interior is intricate and complex. X-ray view, architectural cutaway, anatomical diagram.
**When to use:** "How it works," inside view, teardowns, architecture explanations.
**Example:** An AI chip cut in half showing layers of internal circuitry

### 17. Partial Reveal / Peek
Only a fragment of the subject is visible — the rest is hidden in darkness, behind a wall, or off-frame. Enough to intrigue, not enough to understand. The hidden part is implied to be much larger.
**When to use:** Secrets, mystery, teasers, "you haven't seen the whole picture."
**Example:** A corner of a classified document, most of it in shadow

### 18. Redacted / Censored
A visible document, screen, or image with critical parts blacked out or [REDACTED]. What's visible gives context; what's hidden is what you want to know.
**When to use:** Secrets, censorship, hidden information, "what they won't tell you."
**Example:** An AI research paper with key findings blacked out

### 19. Unwrapping / Peeling
An outer layer being peeled back, removed, or torn away to reveal something different underneath. The surface is one thing; the reality is another.
**When to use:** Exposing truth, looking behind the curtain, "what's really going on," revealing hidden nature.
**Example:** A friendly AI interface being peeled back to reveal dark machinery underneath

## SPREAD / TERRITORY FAMILY

### 20. Map / Spread
A geographic or network visualization showing something spreading across a territory. Color-coded regions, spreading patterns, connection lines. The spread itself is the story.
**When to use:** Global adoption, geographic competition, spread of technology, "how far it's reached."
**Example:** World map with AI adoption spreading like red zones across countries

### 21. Swarm / Multiplication
An overwhelming quantity of identical things filling the frame. Not organized (that's Grid) — organic, spreading, multiplying. Sheer volume is the message.
**When to use:** Replication, exponential growth, being overwhelmed by quantity, "too many to control."
**Example:** Thousands of identical AI bots filling the frame, spilling out of a screen

### 22. Web / Network
Interconnected nodes with visible connections between them. One node might be highlighted or larger. The pattern of connections tells the story — clusters, isolated nodes, central hubs.
**When to use:** Networks, connections, systems, ecosystems, social dynamics, how things relate.
**Example:** AI companies as nodes, connections showing partnerships and dependencies

## FRAMING / PERSPECTIVE FAMILY

### 23. POV / Threshold
First-person perspective looking through a doorway, portal, window, or opening into something beyond. You're AT the boundary, about to step through.
**When to use:** Entering new territory, crossing a threshold, point of no return, discovery.
**Example:** Looking through a door into a glowing AI research lab

### 24. Surveillance / Monitor View
The image framed as if viewed through a security camera, monitor, phone screen, or viewfinder. Interface elements, timestamps, recording indicators. Fourth-wall breaking — you're watching someone watching.
**When to use:** Privacy, surveillance, monitoring, "being watched," AI observation.
**Example:** Security camera view of an AI making decisions, timestamp in corner

### 25. UI / Interface
The entire thumbnail styled as a system interface — error dialog, dashboard, terminal output, notification. Not a photo; a screen. The UI itself is the visual.
**When to use:** Technical warnings, system-level topics, "your computer is telling you something."
**Example:** A system alert screen reading "UNAUTHORIZED PROCESS DETECTED"

### 26. Macro / Extreme Close-up
Zoomed in so close that a familiar object becomes alien and abstract. Texture and detail that's normally invisible becomes the entire image. Disorienting scale.
**When to use:** "What it really looks like," detail-level analysis, microstructure, the invisible world.
**Example:** Extreme close-up of a chip die, looking like an alien city

## METAPHOR / SYMBOL FAMILY

### 27. Single Iconic Object
One bold symbol or object filling the center of the frame. Nothing else. The object IS the concept. Maximum simplicity, maximum impact.
**When to use:** Any topic that can be distilled to one powerful visual symbol.
**Example:** A single red power button (for "should we turn AI off?")

### 28. Visual Metaphor
A literal depiction of a metaphor — something that obviously isn't real but instantly communicates the concept. Creative, sometimes surreal.
**When to use:** Abstract topics that need a concrete visual. "AI arms race" = literal weapons made of code.
**Example:** A house of cards made of AI company logos (for "the AI bubble")

### 29. Absence / Empty Space
A space where something clearly should be but isn't. An empty chair, vacant position, missing piece. The environment shows what was there; the void shows it's gone.
**When to use:** Replacement, loss, "what happens when X is gone," post-human scenarios.
**Example:** An empty desk in an office with a robot walking away in the background

## REFLECTION / REFRACTION FAMILY

### 30. Sunglasses Reflection
A person (or figure) wearing reflective sunglasses. The lenses show something dramatic — a different world, a secret, the real truth. The face is cool and detached; the reflection tells the real story.
**When to use:** Seeing the truth, hidden knowledge, "what they're really looking at," cool reveal.
**Example:** Figure in dark sunglasses, lenses reflecting an AI apocalypse scene

### 31. Mirror / Reflection
A subject looking into a mirror, but the reflection shows something different — a truer self, a future version, or a monstrous alter ego. The gap between reality and reflection IS the concept.
**When to use:** Duality, hidden nature, self-deception, "what you really are," identity crisis.
**Example:** A friendly chatbot interface reflected as a menacing entity in the mirror

### 32. Water Reflection
A scene reflected in water, but the reflection is distorted, darker, or shows a different version. The surface is the dividing line between two realities.
**When to use:** Surface vs depth, calm exterior hiding chaos, dual nature, hidden truth.
**Example:** Calm tech campus reflected as a chaotic dystopia in the water below

### 33. Screen Within Screen
A device screen (phone, laptop, TV) showing something that bleeds into or contrasts with the world around it. The boundary between screen and reality is the tension.
**When to use:** Digital vs physical, virtual leaking into real, "what's on your screen," media influence.
**Example:** Phone screen showing paradise while the hand holding it is in ruins

### 34. Glass/Crystal Refraction
Something viewed through glass, crystal, or a prism — distorted, split into fragments, or color-shifted. The distortion itself is meaningful.
**When to use:** Distorted perception, seeing things wrong, fragmented understanding, multiple perspectives.
**Example:** An AI through a cracked prism, split into rainbow fragments

A miniature world trapped inside a container — snow globe, jar, bottle, terrarium. Something vast reduced to something you can hold. The container implies control or fragility.
**When to use:** Controlled environments, fragile systems, "playing god," contained worlds, artificial ecosystems.
**Example:** A tiny civilization inside a glass jar held by a robotic hand

### 36. Cage / Prison
A subject behind bars, in a cage, or enclosed in a restrictive space. The enclosure is visible and oppressive. The subject may be calm or struggling.
**When to use:** Trapped, restricted, censored, controlled, "can't escape," regulation.
**Example:** An AI entity locked in a glowing red cage, pressing against the bars

### 37. Fish Bowl
A subject inside a transparent bowl or bubble, visible to the outside world but unable to escape. Others may be watching from outside. The bowl distorts the view.
**When to use:** Being watched, living in a bubble, limited perspective, goldfish memory, trapped in plain sight.
**Example:** A human figure inside a fish bowl while AI entities peer in from outside

### 38. Box / Packaging
Something presented as a product — in a box, with packaging, on a shelf. The contrast between the mundane commercial presentation and the dramatic contents is the tension.
**When to use:** Commodification, "it's just a product now," commercialization, unboxing, what's really inside.
**Example:** A black box labeled "SUPERINTELLIGENCE" with red warning tape, half-opened

### 39. Quarantine / Containment Zone
A sealed area with hazmat tape, warning signs, or containment barriers. Something dangerous is being kept inside — or kept out. The barriers are the focus.
**When to use:** Dangerous tech, containment protocols, "should we have released this," safety, biohazard.
**Example:** A server room behind biohazard containment barriers, red warning lights

## EMERGENCE / BREAKING FAMILY

### 40. Breaking Through
Something smashing through a wall, glass, or barrier — mid-impact, with debris flying. The breakthrough is violent and sudden. Whatever was being contained is getting out.
**When to use:** Disruption, breakthrough, escaping control, breaking barriers, unstoppable force.
**Example:** A glowing red fist punching through a concrete wall from the other side

### 41. Hatching / Emerging
Something cracking open to reveal what's inside — an egg, cocoon, shell, pod. The emergence is in progress, partially visible. What's coming out may be beautiful or terrifying.
**When to use:** Birth of something new, transformation complete, "it's alive," evolution, potential.
**Example:** A giant dark egg cracking open with red light spilling from the cracks

### 42. Rising From Below
Something rising up from underground, from water, or from beneath a surface. Only the top is visible — the rest is still submerged and unknown. A sense of massive scale below.
**When to use:** Emerging threats, rising powers, "what's coming," sleeping giants waking up.
**Example:** A colossal AI structure rising from a digital ocean, only the crown visible

### 43. Spilling / Overflowing
A container that can't hold its contents — liquid, objects, data, or creatures pouring out, overflowing the edges. The loss of control is the visual.
**When to use:** Can't contain it, too much, overflow, loss of control, abundance gone wrong.
**Example:** A smartphone overflowing with AI-generated content spilling onto the floor

### 44. Tearing Through
A surface (paper, screen, fabric, reality) being torn open from behind. Hands, claws, or light ripping through the membrane. Something is forcing its way through the fourth wall.
**When to use:** Breaking the illusion, something behind the curtain, reality tearing, invasion.
**Example:** Mechanical fingers tearing through a paper-thin reality to reveal circuitry behind

## HANDS / INTERACTION FAMILY

### 45. Hand Holding Object
A single hand holding, presenting, or offering something significant. The hand could be human, robotic, or monstrous. The object in the hand is the focal point. Close crop, dramatic lighting.
**When to use:** Power, control, offering, "this is what it comes down to," simplifying a complex topic to one symbol.
**Example:** A robotic hand delicately holding a human brain, red backlighting

### 46. Two Hands Reaching
Two hands reaching toward each other — Sistine Chapel style. They may be about to touch, or there's a gap between them. One hand may be human, the other not.
**When to use:** Connection, almost-there, human-AI relationship, cooperation, the gap between.
**Example:** Human hand and AI hand reaching across a digital void, fingertips almost touching

### 47. Hand Crushing / Squeezing
A powerful hand crushing, squeezing, or gripping something fragile. The power dynamic is extreme — the hand is in total control, the object is helpless.
**When to use:** Dominance, destruction, power imbalance, "being crushed by," monopoly power.
**Example:** A massive dark hand crushing a handful of small tech company logos

### 48. Puppet Strings
Something being controlled by strings from above. The puppeteer may or may not be visible. The strings imply hidden control, manipulation, lack of autonomy.
**When to use:** Manipulation, hidden control, "who's really pulling the strings," conspiracy, lack of agency.
**Example:** A human figure on puppet strings, the strings leading up into darkness

### 49. Pulling Apart / Tug of War
Two forces pulling something in opposite directions — stretching, straining, about to tear. The thing in the middle is the victim. The tension is at the breaking point.
**When to use:** Competing interests, being torn between, tension, divided loyalties, about to break.
**Example:** Two giant hands pulling a globe apart, cracks glowing red at the center

## TRANSFORMATION / MORPHING FAMILY

### 50. Half-and-Half
One subject that's literally split down the middle — left half is one thing, right half is another. Not two panels (that's Split Screen) — one single object that IS both things simultaneously.
**When to use:** Dual nature, transformation caught midway, hybrid identity, "part X part Y."
**Example:** A face that's half human, half machine — clean split down the center

### 51. Melting / Dissolving
Something solid melting, dissolving, or dripping away. The form is recognizable but deteriorating. The dissolution could be beautiful or horrifying.
**When to use:** Decay, losing identity, falling apart, instability, "it's not as solid as you think."
**Example:** A corporate logo melting into a pool of red liquid

### 52. Glitch / Corruption
A recognizable image corrupted by digital artifacts — pixel displacement, color channel splitting, data corruption. The original is visible but damaged.
**When to use:** Digital failure, hacked, corrupted, broken technology, unreliable AI, system error.
**Example:** A portrait heavily glitched — face displaced, scan lines, RGB split

### 53. Fossilization / Petrification
Something modern frozen as if it's ancient — fossilized in rock, turned to stone, encased in amber. The juxtaposition of modern subject and ancient medium is the hook.
**When to use:** Already obsolete, outdated, "this will be a relic," preserved in time, legacy tech.
**Example:** A modern smartphone fossilized in stone, like a museum artifact

### 54. Metamorphosis Mid-Transform
A subject actively changing from one form to another — caught in the middle of the transformation. Part old, part new, the transition zone is chaotic and dramatic.
**When to use:** Active change, evolution in progress, "becoming something else," unstable transition.
**Example:** A traditional office desk morphing into a server rack, the transition zone sparking

A balance scale with two items being weighed against each other. One side may be heavier, tipping the scale. The measurement implies a judgment.
**When to use:** Trade-offs, weighing options, cost vs benefit, fairness, "which matters more."
**Example:** A scale with "safety" on one side and "progress" on the other, dramatically tipped

### 56. Scoreboard / Leaderboard

**When to use:** Rankings, competition, "who's winning," performance comparison, sports-style framing.
**Example:** A dark leaderboard with AI company names, top entry glowing red

Something with a visible price tag, value label, or cost indicator. The price could be absurdly high, surprisingly low, or the thing might be marked "PRICELESS" or "FREE."
**When to use:** Cost, value, "what's it really worth," expensive mistakes, hidden costs.
**Example:** A human brain with a price tag reading $0.00, hanging in a dark space

### 58. Gauge / Meter
A dial, meter, or gauge showing a reading — temperature, pressure, danger level. The needle is at a critical position. The instrument frames the subject as measurable and possibly critical.
**When to use:** Danger levels, reaching limits, "how bad is it," pressure, capacity.
**Example:** A danger meter with the needle deep in the red zone, cracking the glass

### 59. Report Card / Grade
Something being graded or rated — with a visible letter grade, score, or pass/fail. The evaluation framing makes any subject feel like it's being judged.
**When to use:** Evaluation, performance review, "how good is it really," grading, rating.
**Example:** An AI model's "report card" with a big red F in the center

**Example:** An AI model laid out on an autopsy table, layers exposed under clinical lighting

A subject framed as a mugshot or wanted poster — height markers, harsh frontal lighting, identifying information. The criminal framing makes anything look suspicious.
**When to use:** Blame, accusations, "the guilty party," calling something out, accountability.
**Example:** A tech CEO in mugshot framing with height markers and harsh flat lighting

### 63. Evidence Board
A detective's evidence board — photos, strings connecting clues, notes, pushpins. The conspiracy/investigation aesthetic. You're piecing together a mystery.

### 64. Magnifying Glass / Inspection
A magnifying glass or loupe zooming in on a specific detail, making it large and impossible to ignore. The rest of the scene is normal-sized; the magnified area is the revelation.

**Example:** An hourglass with glowing red data flowing down, the top chamber nearly empty

### 66. Countdown Clock / Timer
A digital or analog clock/timer showing a specific time — often near zero, midnight, or a deadline. The numbers are the focal point. The countdown framing creates tension.
**When to use:** Deadlines, "time's up," doomsday clocks, countdowns, urgency, moments before.
**Example:** A massive digital countdown timer at 00:00:03 in red, dark background

### 67. Calendar / Date
A specific date circled, crossed out, or highlighted on a calendar. The date itself is the drama — what happens then? The rest of the calendar provides context.
**When to use:** Predictions, deadlines, "mark this date," anniversaries, scheduled events.
**Example:** A calendar with a date circled in red, everything after it blacked out

### 68. Fossil Record / Geological Layers
Layers of time stacked vertically like geological strata. Each layer represents an era. You can read time by reading depth. The current era is the thin top layer.

**Example:** Geological cross-section with tech eras as layers, AI as the thin top stratum

**When to use:** System failure, crash, "it's broken," critical errors, tech collapse, ironic framing.
**Example:** A blue screen of death with "ARTIFICIAL_GENERAL_INTELLIGENCE_EXCEPTION"

### 70. Loading / Progress Bar
A progress bar or loading indicator that's almost complete — or stuck. The anticipation of "what loads when it finishes" creates tension. The bar is the entire visual.
**When to use:** Waiting, anticipation, "almost there," progress toward something big, patience.

A phone notification, system alert, or popup message as the visual. The message content IS the concept. The casual notification format makes dramatic content feel immediate and personal.
**When to use:** Breaking news, personal impact, "this just happened," alerts, warnings.

A terminal or command-line interface filling the frame. The commands or output tell the story. The hacker/tech aesthetic makes anything feel technical and serious.

A search engine results page where the results themselves tell the story. The familiar search UI makes the content feel real, immediate, and discoverable.
